Phrasal Verbs

Concept: A phrasal verb is a combination of a verb and a preposition or adverb, creating a meaning different from the original verb.

50 Essential Phrasal Verbs:

  • Account for: to explain the reason for something.
    Example: He could not account for his absence.
  • Ask for: to request something.
    Example: She asked for a glass of water.
  • Back up: to support someone or make a copy of a file.
    Example: My friends will always back me up.
  • Blow up: to explode or to inflate.
    Example: The bomb blew up. Please blow up these balloons.
  • Break down: to stop working (machine) or lose emotional control.
    Example: His car broke down on the highway.
  • Break up: to end a relationship.
    Example: They decided to break up after five years.
  • Bring about: to cause something to happen.
    Example: The new policy brought about significant changes.
  • Bring up: to raise a child or to mention a topic.
    Example: She was brought up by her grandparents.
  • Call off: to cancel an event.
    Example: They had to call off the meeting due to the storm.
  • Carry on: to continue doing something.
    Example: Despite the difficulties, they decided to carry on.
  • Catch up: to reach the same level as someone else.
    Example: You need to work hard to catch up with the rest of the class.
  • Come across: to find something or meet someone by chance.
    Example: I came across an old photograph while cleaning.
  • Come up with: to think of an idea or a plan.
    Example: She came up with a brilliant idea for the project.
  • Cut down on: to reduce the amount of something.
    Example: The doctor advised him to cut down on sugar.
  • Do away with: to abolish or get rid of something.
    Example: The school decided to do away with the old uniform.
  • Drop out: to quit a course or school.
    Example: He had to drop out of college due to financial problems.
  • Fall apart: to break into pieces or fail.
    Example: The old book is falling apart.
  • Figure out: to understand or solve something.
    Example: I can't figure out how to solve this puzzle.
  • Find out: to discover a fact or piece of information.
    Example: We need to find out the truth.
  • Get along with: to have a friendly relationship with someone.
    Example: She gets along with all her colleagues.
  • Get away with: to escape blame or punishment for wrongdoing.
    Example: He thought he could get away with cheating, but he was caught.
  • Get over: to recover from an illness or a disappointment.
    Example: It took her a long time to get over the breakup.
  • Give in: to surrender or yield.
    Example: He finally gave in to their demands.
  • Give up: to stop trying; to quit a habit.
    Example: You should give up smoking.
  • Go on: to continue or to happen.
    Example: The show must go on. What's going on here?
  • Go through: to experience a difficult situation.
    Example: She has gone through a lot in her life.
  • Hand in: to submit an assignment.
    Example: Please hand in your essays by Friday.
  • Hold on: to wait for a short time.
    Example: Please hold on; the manager will be with you shortly.
  • Keep on: to continue doing something.
    Example: Just keep on trying and you will succeed.
  • Let down: to disappoint someone.
    Example: I promised to help him, and I won't let him down.
  • Look after: to take care of someone/something.
    Example: She needs to look after her sick mother.
  • Look forward to: to anticipate something with pleasure.
    Example: I am looking forward to the holidays.
  • Look into: to investigate.
    Example: The police will look into the matter.
  • Look up: to search for information in a book or online.
    Example: If you don't know the meaning, look it up in a dictionary.
  • Make up: to invent a story or to reconcile after an argument.
    Example: He made up a story. They finally made up after their fight.
  • Pass away: a polite way to say 'to die'.
    Example: Her grandfather passed away last year.
  • Pass out: to faint or lose consciousness.
    Example: He passed out from the heat.
  • Point out: to draw attention to something.
    Example: He pointed out the mistakes in my report.
  • Put off: to postpone.
    Example: Never put off until tomorrow what you can do today.
  • Put up with: to tolerate something unpleasant.
    Example: I can't put up with his rude behavior anymore.
  • Run into: to meet someone unexpectedly.
    Example: I ran into an old friend at the supermarket.
  • Run out of: to have no more of something left.
    Example: We have run out of milk.
  • Set up: to establish or arrange.
    Example: They set up a new business.
  • Show up: to arrive or appear.
    Example: He didn't show up for the meeting.
  • Take after: to resemble a family member.
    Example: He takes after his father.
  • Take off: to leave the ground (airplane) or to become successful quickly.
    Example: The plane will take off in ten minutes.
  • Take over: to take control of something.
    Example: The new manager will take over next week.
  • Turn down: to reject an offer.
    Example: She had to turn down the job offer.
  • Turn up: to appear suddenly or to increase volume.
    Example: He finally turned up at the party. Can you turn up the music?
  • Work out: to exercise or to find a solution.
    Example: I try to work out every morning. We need to work out a plan.

Confusing Words (Paronyms & Homophones)

Concept: These are pairs of words that look or sound similar but have different meanings, making them common traps in exams.

50 Common Pairs:

  • Accept / Except: Accept (v) - to receive. Except (prep) - not including.
    I accept your apology. Everyone is here except John.
  • Advice / Advise: Advice (n) - a recommendation. Advise (v) - to give a recommendation.
    He gave me some good advice. I would advise you to study harder.
  • Affect / Effect: Affect (v) - to influence. Effect (n) - a result.
    The weather will affect our plans. The effect of the medicine was immediate.
  • Allusion / Illusion: Allusion (n) - an indirect reference. Illusion (n) - a false perception.
    The novel contains an allusion to Shakespeare. The oasis was an illusion.
  • A lot / Allot: A lot (phrase) - many. Allot (v) - to assign a share.
    I have a lot of work. Please allot the tasks to the team.
  • Altar / Alter: Altar (n) - a holy table. Alter (v) - to change.
    The priest stood at the altar. We need to alter our plans.
  • Ascent / Assent: Ascent (n) - the act of climbing up. Assent (n/v) - agreement/to agree.
    The ascent of Mount Everest is difficult. The manager gave his assent to the project.
  • Bare / Bear: Bare (adj) - uncovered. Bear (v/n) - to endure or an animal.
    The walls were bare. I cannot bear this pain. A bear lives in the forest.
  • Berth / Birth: Berth (n) - a bed on a train/ship. Birth (n) - being born.
    He booked a lower berth. We celebrated the baby's birth.
  • Beside / Besides: Beside (prep) - next to. Besides (prep/adv) - in addition to.
    He sat beside me. Besides English, she speaks French.
  • Brake / Break: Brake (n/v) - a device to stop. Break (v/n) - to shatter or a pause.
    The car's brakes failed. Be careful not to break the glass.
  • Canvas / Canvass: Canvas (n) - a strong cloth. Canvass (v) - to seek votes or opinions.
    The painting is on a canvas. They will canvass the neighborhood for support.
  • Capital / Capitol: Capital (n) - main city, wealth. Capitol (n) - a government building.
    Delhi is the capital of India. The US Congress meets at the Capitol building.
  • Cite / Site / Sight: Cite (v) - to quote. Site (n) - a location. Sight (n) - vision.
    You must cite your sources. This is the site for the new building. His sight is getting weaker.
  • Coarse / Course: Coarse (adj) - rough. Course (n) - a path or a series of lessons.
    The sandpaper has a coarse texture. I am taking a new course.
  • Complement / Compliment: Complement (v/n) - to complete. Compliment (v/n) - to praise.
    The sauce complements the dish. She gave me a nice compliment.
  • Conscience / Conscious: Conscience (n) - moral sense. Conscious (adj) - aware.
    His conscience was clear. He was conscious of the danger.
  • Council / Counsel: Council (n) - an advisory group. Counsel (n/v) - advice/to advise.
    The city council held a meeting. I need your counsel.
  • Dairy / Diary: Dairy (n) - place for milk products. Diary (n) - a personal journal.
    We buy milk from the dairy. She writes in her diary every night.
  • Desert / Dessert: Desert (n/v) - dry land/to abandon. Dessert (n) - a sweet dish.
    The Sahara is a large desert. He ate ice cream for dessert.
  • Discreet / Discrete: Discreet (adj) - careful, secretive. Discrete (adj) - separate, distinct.
    Please be discreet about this. The project has several discrete parts.
  • Elicit / Illicit: Elicit (v) - to draw out a response. Illicit (adj) - illegal.
    The lawyer tried to elicit a confession. He was arrested for illicit activities.
  • Eminent / Imminent: Eminent (adj) - famous. Imminent (adj) - about to happen.
    He is an eminent scientist. The storm is imminent.
  • Ensure / Insure: Ensure (v) - to make sure something happens. Insure (v) - to protect financially.
    Please ensure the doors are locked. You should insure your car.
  • Farther / Further: Farther (adv) - physical distance. Further (adv) - metaphorical distance or 'in addition'.
    I cannot walk any farther. Let's discuss this further.
  • Flair / Flare: Flair (n) - a natural talent. Flare (v/n) - to burn brightly or a burst of light.
    She has a flair for design. The fire began to flare up.
  • Formally / Formerly: Formally (adv) - in a formal manner. Formerly (adv) - in the past.
    He was dressed formally. He was formerly a teacher.
  • Heard / Herd: Heard (v) - past tense of hear. Herd (n) - a group of animals.
    I heard a strange noise. A herd of cattle was grazing.
  • Hoard / Horde: Hoard (v/n) - to store up secretly. Horde (n) - a large crowd.
    The dragon would hoard its gold. A horde of tourists arrived.
  • Its / It's: Its (possessive) - shows ownership. It's - contraction of 'it is' or 'it has'.
    The dog wagged its tail. It's a beautiful day.
  • Loath / Loathe: Loath (adj) - reluctant. Loathe (v) - to hate.
    I am loath to admit my mistake. I absolutely loathe spiders.
  • Lose / Loose: Lose (v) - to misplace or not win. Loose (adj) - not tight.
    Don't lose your keys. This shirt is too loose for me.
  • Moral / Morale: Moral (adj/n) - related to principles of right and wrong. Morale (n) - confidence and enthusiasm.
    The story has a good moral. The team's morale was high after the victory.
  • Peak / Peek / Pique: Peak (n) - the top point. Peek (v) - a quick look. Pique (v) - to arouse interest.
    They reached the mountain peak. He took a quick peek. The story will pique your curiosity.
  • Personal / Personnel: Personal (adj) - private. Personnel (n) - staff or employees.
    This is a personal matter. The company's personnel are highly skilled.
  • Pore / Pour: Pore (v/n) - to study carefully or a tiny opening. Pour (v) - to make a liquid flow.
    She would pore over her books. Please pour me some water.
  • Precede / Proceed: Precede (v) - to come before. Proceed (v) - to go forward.
    The introduction will precede the main chapter. Please proceed.
  • Prescribe / Proscribe: Prescribe (v) - to recommend (medicine). Proscribe (v) - to forbid or ban.
    The doctor will prescribe some antibiotics. The law will proscribe such activities.
  • Principal / Principle: Principal (n/adj) - head of a school, main. Principle (n) - a rule or belief.
    The principal of the school gave a speech. He is a man of strong principles.
  • Quiet / Quite: Quiet (adj) - silent. Quite (adv) - very or completely.
    Please be quiet. The test was quite difficult.
  • Stationary / Stationery: Stationary (adj) - not moving. Stationery (n) - writing materials.
    The car was stationary. I need to buy some stationery.
  • Than / Then: Than (conj) - for comparison. Then (adv) - at that time.
    He is taller than his brother. Finish your work, and then you can play.
  • Their / There / They're: Their (possessive) - ownership. There (adv) - a place. They're - contraction of 'they are'.
    They submitted their work. Put the book there. They're coming soon.
  • To / Too / Two: To (prep) - direction. Too (adv) - also or excessively. Two (number) - 2.
    I am going to the store. It is too hot. I have two pens.
  • Waist / Waste: Waist (n) - middle part of the body. Waste (v/n) - to use carelessly.
    He measured his waist. Don't waste your time.
  • Weather / Whether: Weather (n) - atmospheric conditions. Whether (conj) - introducing a choice.
    The weather is nice. I don't know whether to stay or go.
  • Who's / Whose: Who's - contraction of 'who is' or 'who has'. Whose (pronoun) - shows possession.
    Who's at the door? Whose book is this?
  • Your / You're: Your (possessive) - ownership. You're - contraction of 'you are'.
    This is your pen. You're a good student.
  • Cent / Scent / Sent: Cent (n) - a coin. Scent (n) - a smell. Sent (v) - past tense of send.
    It costs fifty cents. The flower has a lovely scent. I sent the email.
  • Complementary / Complimentary: Complementary (adj) - completing. Complimentary (adj) - free or praising.
    The two colors are complementary. The hotel provides a complimentary breakfast.

One-Word Substitution

Concept: Replacing a phrase or a full sentence with a single, precise word.

50 Essential Examples:

  • A person who loves books: Bibliophile
  • Something that cannot be avoided or prevented: Inevitable
  • A place where weapons are stored: Arsenal
  • A government by the people, for the people: Democracy
  • A life history of a person written by himself: Autobiography
  • A life history of a person written by another person: Biography
  • Something that is no longer in use: Obsolete
  • A remedy for all diseases or problems: Panacea
  • That which cannot be read or deciphered: Illegible
  • A person who can do anything for money: Mercenary
  • Something that can be heard: Audible
  • Something that can be seen: Visible
  • A place where birds are kept: Aviary
  • A place where bees are kept: Apiary
  • The study of the origin and history of words: Etymology
  • One who does not believe in the existence of God: Atheist
  • One who believes in the existence of God: Theist
  • A person who can speak many languages: Polyglot
  • A person who is new to a profession or activity: Novice / Tyro
  • A person who looks at the bright side of things: Optimist
  • A person who looks at the dark side of things: Pessimist
  • One who loves all mankind: Philanthropist
  • One who hates mankind: Misanthrope
  • A person who eats human flesh: Cannibal
  • A person who eats only vegetables: Vegetarian
  • A person who eats meat: Carnivore
  • A person who eats everything: Omnivore
  • A person who travels to a holy place: Pilgrim
  • One who is present everywhere: Omnipresent
  • One who knows everything: Omniscient
  • One who is all-powerful: Omnipotent
  • A person who leaves his own country to settle in another: Emigrant
  • A person who comes to one country from another to settle: Immigrant
  • One who can use both hands equally well: Ambidextrous
  • A person who does something for pleasure, not as a profession: Amateur
  • Fear of water: Hydrophobia
  • Fear of heights: Acrophobia
  • A list of books in a library: Catalogue
  • A group of stars: Constellation
  • The scientific study of earthquakes: Seismology
  • A period of ten years: Decade
  • A period of one hundred years: Century
  • A handwritten book: Manuscript
  • Something that is sure to happen: Certain
  • A speech made without preparation: Extempore
  • Something that can be believed: Credible
  • Something that cannot be believed: Incredible
  • The killing of one person by another: Homicide
  • A place for sick people who need long periods of recovery: Sanatorium
  • An animal that lives in groups: Gregarious
