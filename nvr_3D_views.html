View of a 3D Object from Different Angles

Theory and Solving Techniques

What is "View of a 3D Object from Different Angles"?

This type of non-verbal reasoning question tests your spatial visualization skills. You are shown an isometric (3D) view of an object, and you must identify its correct orthographic (2D) view from a specific angle: usually the Top View, Front View, or Side View.

  • Top View: Imagine you are a bird flying directly above the object. What flat shape would you see? This is also called the "plan view".
  • Front View: Imagine you are standing directly in front of the object (often indicated by an arrow). What flat shape would you see? This is also called the "elevation".
  • Side View: Imagine you are standing directly to the side of the object (usually the right or left side). What flat shape would you see?

How to Solve These Questions

  1. Understand the Question: Carefully read which view is being asked for (Top, Front, or Side). If an arrow is present, it almost always indicates the "Front" direction.
  2. Focus on One Face at a Time: Start by visualizing the faces you would see from that specific angle. Ignore the depth. For a top view, you only see the top surfaces. For a front view, you only see the front-facing surfaces.
  3. Count the Surfaces/Blocks: Count how many distinct flat surfaces or blocks are visible from that angle. This helps you quickly eliminate options that have the wrong number of shapes.
  4. Check for Alignment and Adjacency: Observe how the different surfaces are positioned relative to each other. Are they side-by-side? Is one above the other? The 2D view must maintain this relative positioning.
  5. Look for Hidden Edges: Sometimes, an edge is hidden behind another part of the object. In technical drawings, these are shown with dashed lines. In most reasoning tests, hidden lines are omitted. However, be aware that a visible line in a 2D view might represent an edge that is further back in the 3D object. A change in level creates a line.
  6. Eliminate Incorrect Options: Use the points above to systematically rule out the wrong answers. It's often easier to spot why an option is wrong than to confirm the right one immediately. Does an option show a view from a different angle? Does it have extra or missing lines/shapes?
