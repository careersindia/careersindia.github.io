Number Series Completion

Core Concept: The Difference Method

Question

What is the most effective first step to identify the pattern in a number series?

Answer

Calculate the difference between consecutive terms. If the first layer of differences doesn't reveal a pattern, calculate the difference of the differences (a "double difference").

Explanation

The speaker explains that most series problems are based on a hidden arithmetic or geometric progression. Finding the difference between the numbers is the fastest way to uncover this pattern.

  • Single Difference: The differences form a simple pattern (e.g., all 5s, or 2, 4, 6, 8...).
  • Double Difference: The first set of differences is not simple, but the differences of that set are constant or follow a simple pattern. This often reveals a pattern based on squares or cubes.

Important Notes: The speaker strongly advises, "Don't just stare at the series and try to guess. The first thing you must do is pick up your pen and find the difference. 90% of the time, the logic is hidden in the gap between the numbers. If the numbers are increasing very fast, think multiplication or squares/cubes. If they are increasing slowly, think addition."

Question 1: Constant Difference (Arithmetic Progression)

Question

2, 5, 8, 11, 14, ?

Answer

17

Explanation

We find the difference between each consecutive term:

  • 5 - 2 = 3
  • 8 - 5 = 3
  • 11 - 8 = 3
  • 14 - 11 = 3

The pattern is a constant addition of 3. Therefore, the next term is 14 + 3 = 17.

Question 2: Increasing Difference

Question

3, 7, 13, 21, 31, ?

Answer

43

Explanation

We find the first layer of differences:

  • 7 - 3 = 4
  • 13 - 7 = 6
  • 21 - 13 = 8
  • 31 - 21 = 10

The differences are increasing by 2 each time (4, 6, 8, 10...). The next difference should be 12. Therefore, the next term in the main series is 31 + 12 = 43.

Question 3: Double Difference (Square Pattern)

Question

1, 2, 6, 15, 31, ?

Answer

56

Explanation

The first layer of differences is calculated:

  • 2 - 1 = 1
  • 6 - 2 = 4
  • 15 - 6 = 9
  • 31 - 15 = 16

The differences (1, 4, 9, 16) are perfect squares (1², 2², 3², 4²). The next difference must be 5² = 25. Therefore, the next term is 31 + 25 = 56.

Important Notes: "As soon as you see the differences 1, 4, 9, 16, your brain should immediately recognize these as squares," the speaker says. "This is why it's crucial to be familiar with squares up to at least 20. Recognizing these patterns quickly is the key to solving series problems fast."

Question 4: Cube Pattern

Question

1, 9, 36, 100, ?

Answer

225

Explanation

This series is tricky because the numbers themselves are squares, but the base of the squares follows a pattern. The series can be rewritten as:

  • 1 = 1² (where the base is 1³, or just 1)
  • 9 = 3² (where the base is 1+2 = 3)
  • 36 = 6² (where the base is 1+2+3 = 6)
  • 100 = 10² (where the base is 1+2+3+4 = 10)

The pattern is the square of the sum of consecutive natural numbers. The next term's base will be 1+2+3+4+5 = 15. Therefore, the next number in the series is 15² = 225.

Important Notes: This example illustrates a more complex, multi-layered pattern. The speaker notes, "Sometimes the pattern is not in the numbers themselves, but in the *roots* of the numbers. If you see many perfect squares, rewrite them as squares and then look for a pattern in the base numbers."

Question 5: Multiplication and Addition Pattern

Question

5, 11, 23, 47, 95, ?

Answer

191

Explanation

The numbers are roughly doubling. This suggests a pattern involving multiplication by 2. Let's test this:

  • 5 * 2 = 10. To get 11, we add 1. (Pattern: *2 + 1)
  • 11 * 2 = 22. To get 23, we add 1. (Pattern: *2 + 1)
  • 23 * 2 = 46. To get 47, we add 1. (Pattern: *2 + 1)
  • 47 * 2 = 94. To get 95, we add 1. (Pattern: *2 + 1)

The consistent pattern is "multiply by 2, then add 1". Applying this to the last term: 95 * 2 + 1 = 190 + 1 = 191.
