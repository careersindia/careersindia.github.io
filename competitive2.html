7. Phrasal Verbs

Concept: A phrasal verb is a combination of a verb and a preposition or adverb (or both), which together have a meaning different from the original verb.

20 Common Phrasal Verbs:

  • Look after: to take care of someone/something.
    Example: She needs to look after her younger brother.
  • Look into: to investigate or examine.
    Example: The police will look into the matter.
  • Break down: to stop working (for a machine) or to lose emotional control.
    Example: His car broke down on the highway. She broke down in tears.
  • Call off: to cancel an event.
    Example: They had to call off the meeting due to the storm.
  • Give up: to stop trying; to surrender.
    Example: Never give up on your dreams.
  • Put off: to postpone.
    Example: He decided to put off the trip until next month.
  • Turn down: to reject an offer.
    Example: She had to turn down the job offer because the salary was too low.
  • Take off: to leave the ground (for an airplane) or to become successful quickly.
    Example: The plane will take off in ten minutes. Her career really took off.
  • Bring up: to raise a child or to mention a topic.
    Example: She was brought up by her grandparents. Don't bring up that topic again.
  • Come across: to find something or meet someone by chance.
    Example: I came across an old photograph while cleaning my room.
  • Get over: to recover from an illness or a disappointment.
    Example: It took him a long time to get over the flu.
  • Hold on: to wait for a short time.
    Example: Please hold on while I transfer your call.
  • Run out of: to have no more of something left.
    Example: We have run out of milk; I need to buy some.
  • Set up: to establish or arrange something (a business, a meeting).
    Example: They plan to set up a new company next year.
  • Back up: to support someone or to make a copy of a file.
    Example: My friends will always back me up. You should back up your computer files.
  • Carry on: to continue doing something.
    Example: Despite the difficulties, they decided to carry on with the project.
  • Find out: to discover a fact or piece of information.
    Example: I need to find out what time the train leaves.
  • Go on: to happen or to continue.
    Example: What is going on here? The show must go on.
  • Make up: to invent a story or to reconcile after an argument.
    Example: He made up an excuse for being late. They finally made up after their fight.
  • Work out: to exercise or to find a solution to a problem.
    Example: I try to work out at the gym three times a week. We need to work out our differences.

8. Idioms & Phrases

Concept: An idiom is a phrase whose meaning cannot be understood from the ordinary meaning of its words. The meaning is figurative.

20 Common Idioms:

  • Bite the bullet: to endure a difficult situation with courage.
    Example: I had to bite the bullet and work extra hours to finish the project on time.
  • A blessing in disguise: a good thing that seemed bad at first.
    Example: Losing my old job was a blessing in disguise because I found a much better one.
  • Spill the beans: to reveal a secret.
    Example: We were planning a surprise party, but my little sister spilled the beans.
  • Break the ice: to initiate a conversation in a social setting.
    Example: He told a funny joke to break the ice at the start of the meeting.
  • Once in a blue moon: something that happens very rarely.
    Example: He lives abroad, so we see him only once in a blue moon.
  • A piece of cake: something that is very easy to do.
    Example: For an expert, solving that puzzle was a piece of cake.
  • Let the cat out of the bag: to reveal a secret accidentally.
    Example: I wanted to keep the gift a surprise, but my friend let the cat out of the bag.
  • Hit the nail on the head: to describe a situation exactly.
    Example: You hit the nail on the head when you said he was a perfectionist.
  • Cost an arm and a leg: to be very expensive.
    Example: The repairs for his imported car cost an arm and a leg.
  • The ball is in your court: it is your turn to make a decision or take the next step.
    Example: I've made my offer; now the ball is in your court.
  • Go the extra mile: to make a special effort.
    Example: She always goes the extra mile to help her students.
  • Feel under the weather: to feel ill or sick.
    Example: I'm feeling a bit under the weather today, so I won't be coming to the office.
  • Jump on the bandwagon: to join a popular activity or trend.
    Example: After the team won a few games, everyone started to jump on the bandwagon.
  • A drop in the ocean: a very small amount compared to what is needed.
    Example: The money they raised was just a drop in the ocean compared to the total amount required.
  • Actions speak louder than words: what you do is more important than what you say.
    Example: He keeps promising to change, but actions speak louder than words.
  • To cut corners: to do something poorly to save time or money.
    Example: The company went out of business because they were always cutting corners on quality.
  • Add fuel to the fire: to make a bad situation worse.
    Example: His angry comments only added fuel to the fire.
  • Every cloud has a silver lining: there is something good in every bad situation.
    Example: I was disappointed to lose the match, but every cloud has a silver lining as I learned a lot from it.
  • Hit the books: to study hard.
    Example: I have a big exam tomorrow, so I need to hit the books tonight.
  • On the same page: to be in agreement or have the same understanding.
    Example: Before we proceed, let's make sure everyone is on the same page.

9. One-Word Substitution

Concept: Replacing a phrase or a full sentence with a single word without changing its meaning.

20 Common Examples:

  • A person who loves books: Bibliophile
  • Something that cannot be avoided: Inevitable
  • A person who can speak many languages: Polyglot
  • A place where animals are kept in their natural environment: Zoo or Sanctuary
  • A government by the people: Democracy
  • One who does not believe in the existence of God: Atheist
  • A life history of a person written by himself: Autobiography
  • A life history of a person written by another: Biography
  • Something that is capable of being heard: Audible
  • A person who eats human flesh: Cannibal
  • A remedy for all diseases: Panacea
  • One who is present everywhere: Omnipresent
  • One who knows everything: Omniscient
  • A person who is new to a profession: Novice or Tyro
  • Something that is no longer in use: Obsolete
  • A person who looks at the bright side of things: Optimist
  • A person who looks at the dark side of things: Pessimist
  • A list of books available in a library: Catalogue
  • That which cannot be read: Illegible
  • A person who can do anything for money: Mercenary

10. Confusing Words (Paronyms & Homophones)

Concept: These are pairs of words that look or sound similar but have different meanings, making them common traps in exams.

20 Common Pairs:

  • Affect / Effect: Affect (v) means to influence. Effect (n) means a result.
    Example: The weather will affect our plans. The effect of the medicine was immediate.
  • Principle / Principal: Principle (n) means a rule or belief. Principal (n/adj) means the head of a school or the main amount of money.
    Example: He is a man of strong principles. The principal of the school addressed the students.
  • Complement / Compliment: Complement (v/n) means to complete or enhance something. Compliment (v/n) means to praise.
    Example: The sauce complements the dish perfectly. She gave me a nice compliment.
  • Accept / Except: Accept (v) means to receive. Except (prep) means 'not including'.
    Example: I accept your apology. Everyone is here except John.
  • Assure / Ensure / Insure: Assure (v) is to promise someone. Ensure (v) is to make sure something happens. Insure (v) is to protect financially.
    Example: I assure you, I will be on time. Please ensure all the doors are locked. You should insure your car against theft.
  • Elicit / Illicit: Elicit (v) means to draw out a response. Illicit (adj) means illegal.
    Example: The lawyer tried to elicit a confession. He was arrested for illicit activities.
  • Adverse / Averse: Adverse (adj) means harmful or unfavorable. Averse (adj) means having a strong dislike.
    Example: The drug has some adverse side effects. He is averse to taking risks.
  • Discrete / Discreet: Discrete (adj) means separate and distinct. Discreet (adj) means careful and secretive.
    Example: The project was broken down into several discrete tasks. Please be discreet about this information.
  • Than / Then: Than (conj) is used for comparison. Then (adv) refers to a point in time.
    Example: He is taller than his brother. First I will finish my work, and then I will go out.
  • Allusion / Illusion: Allusion (n) is an indirect reference. Illusion (n) is a false perception.
    Example: The novel contains an allusion to Shakespeare. The oasis in the desert was just an illusion.
  • Advice / Advise: Advice (n) is a recommendation. Advise (v) is the act of giving a recommendation.
    Example: He gave me some good advice. I would advise you to study harder.
  • Stationary / Stationery: Stationary (adj) means not moving. Stationery (n) refers to writing materials like pens and paper.
    Example: The car was stationary at the red light. I need to buy some stationery for the office.
  • Its / It's: Its (possessive) shows ownership. It's is a contraction of 'it is' or 'it has'.
    Example: The dog wagged its tail. It's a beautiful day.
  • There / Their / They're: There (adv) indicates a place. Their (possessive) shows ownership. They're is a contraction of 'they are'.
    Example: Put the book over there. The students submitted their assignments. They're coming to the party.
  • Lose / Loose: Lose (v) means to misplace or not win. Loose (adj) means not tight.
    Example: Don't lose your keys. This shirt is too loose for me.
  • Weather / Whether: Weather (n) refers to atmospheric conditions. Whether (conj) introduces a choice.
    Example: The weather is pleasant today. I don't know whether to go or stay.
  • Desert / Dessert: Desert (n/v) is a dry land or to abandon. Dessert (n) is a sweet dish.
    Example: The Sahara is a large desert. He ate ice cream for dessert.
  • Precede / Proceed: Precede (v) means to come before. Proceed (v) means to go forward.
    Example: The introduction will precede the main chapter. Please proceed with your presentation.
  • Cite / Site / Sight: Cite (v) is to quote. Site (n) is a location. Sight (n) is the ability to see.
    Example: You must cite your sources. This is the site for the new building. His sight is getting weaker.
  • Eminent / Imminent: Eminent (adj) means famous and respected. Imminent (adj) means about to happen.
    Example: He is an eminent scientist. The storm is imminent.

11. Reading Comprehension (RC) Strategies

Concept: RC tests your ability to read, understand, and analyze a given passage. Strategy is as important as reading speed.

Key Strategies & Solved Example

  • 1. Skim the Passage First: Quickly read the first and last paragraphs, and the first sentence of other paragraphs to get the main idea and tone.
  • 2. Read the Questions Next: Read the questions (but not the options) before reading the passage in detail. This helps you know what to look for.
  • 3. Active Reading: Read the passage carefully now, underlining keywords and making mental notes related to the questions.
  • 4. Identify Question Types:
    • Main Idea/Title: Asks for the central theme. The answer is usually in the first or last paragraph.
    • Direct Questions: The answer is explicitly stated in the passage. Find the keyword in the passage to locate it.
    • Inference Questions: The answer is not directly stated but is strongly implied. Look for clues and draw a logical conclusion.
    • Vocabulary Questions: Asks for the meaning of a word in the context of the passage.
  • 5. Elimination Technique: For multiple-choice questions, eliminate the options that are clearly wrong, too extreme, or not mentioned in the passage.
Solved Example:
Passage: The greatest challenge of the 21st century is climate change. The burning of fossil fuels releases greenhouse gases, which trap heat in the atmosphere, leading to a rise in global temperatures. This phenomenon, known as global warming, has far-reaching effects, including rising sea levels and more extreme weather events. While renewable energy sources like solar and wind are promising alternatives, their widespread adoption faces significant economic and political hurdles.

Q: The primary purpose of the passage is to...
(A) advocate for renewable energy.
(B) describe the effects of global warming.
(C) outline the main aspects of climate change.
(D) criticize the use of fossil fuels.

Explanation: Option (C) is the best answer. The passage introduces climate change as the main challenge (sentence 1), explains its cause (fossil fuels), its mechanism (global warming), its effects (rising sea levels), and the challenges to its solution (renewable energy hurdles). It covers all these aspects, making it a general outline. The other options are too specific or not the main focus.

12. Para Jumbles (Sentence Rearrangement)

Concept: You are given a set of jumbled sentences that, when arranged correctly, form a coherent paragraph.

Key Strategies & Solved Example

  • 1. Find the Opening Sentence: Look for a sentence that introduces a person, concept, or topic. It is usually a general statement and will not start with pronouns like 'he', 'it', 'they' or conjunctions like 'but', 'so'.
  • 2. Identify Mandatory Pairs: Look for sentences that have a clear connection.
    • Pronoun-Antecedent: A sentence with a pronoun (e.g., 'he', 'its') must follow the sentence that introduces the noun it refers to.
    • Cause and Effect: A sentence describing a cause will come before the sentence describing its effect.
    • Chronological Order: Arrange events in the order they happened.
  • 3. Find the Concluding Sentence: Look for a sentence that summarizes the paragraph or provides a final thought. It often starts with words like 'Thus', 'Therefore', 'Hence'.
  • 4. Use the Options (if available): Check the given options to see which sequence makes the most logical sense.
Solved Example:
A. He was a great leader who was loved by his people.
B. This kingdom was located in the fertile plains of the north.
C. Ashoka was one of the greatest emperors of India.
D. There was once a vast kingdom known as Magadha.

Correct Sequence: DBCA

Explanation:
1. D is the best opening sentence as it introduces the broad topic of a kingdom, 'Magadha'.
2. B logically follows D because 'This kingdom' directly refers to 'Magadha' mentioned in D. So, DB is a mandatory pair.
3. C introduces the specific character, 'Ashoka'. While it's a good introductory sentence, it's more specific than D. It fits well after the general setting has been established.
4. A must follow C because the pronoun 'He' clearly refers to 'Ashoka' introduced in C. So, CA is a mandatory pair. 5. Combining the pairs gives us the logical flow: General setting (DB) -> Specific character (C) -> Detail about the character (A). The correct sequence is DBCA.

13. Cloze Test

Concept: A Cloze Test is a passage where some words have been removed. You need to fill in the blanks with the most appropriate words from the given options.

Key Strategies & Solved Example

  • 1. Read the Entire Passage First: Get an overall idea of the passage's topic, tone (positive, negative, neutral), and tense (past, present, future).
  • 2. Focus on Context: Read the sentence before and after the blank to understand the context. The correct word must fit logically and grammatically.
  • 3. Use Grammar Rules:
    • Check for **Subject-Verb Agreement**.
    • Look for the correct **preposition** or **conjunction** that fits the context.
    • Ensure the **tense** of the verb is consistent with the rest of the passage.
  • 4. Use Vocabulary & Collocation: Some blanks test vocabulary. Choose the word with the most appropriate meaning. Look for collocations (words that are often used together, like 'heavy rain').
  • 5. Eliminate Wrong Options: If you are unsure, eliminate the options that are grammatically incorrect or do not fit the context.
Solved Example:
The rise of artificial intelligence is one of the most significant developments of our time. It has the __(1)__ to revolutionize industries from healthcare to transportation. However, many experts are __(2)__ about its potential risks.

Q1. (A) potent (B) ability (C) potential (D) power
Q2. (A) concerned (B) happy (C) careless (D) excited

Explanation:
1. For blank (1), the sentence structure "has the ___ to" requires a noun. 'Potential' means the capacity to develop into something in the future, which fits the context of revolutionizing industries perfectly. So, (C) potential is the correct answer. 'Potent' is an adjective, 'ability' is usually followed by 'to do', and 'power' is less specific here.
2. For blank (2), the word 'However' indicates a contrast. The first sentence is positive ('revolutionize'), so the second sentence must express a contrasting, negative idea. The word 'risks' confirms this. 'Concerned' means worried, which fits perfectly. So, (A) concerned is the correct answer.
