PARA JUMBLES (SENTENCE REARRANGEMENT)

  • Definition & Concept
  • Solving Strategies
  • 100 Solved Examples

Definition & Concept

Concept: Para Jumbles are a set of related sentences that have been mixed up or "jumbled." The goal is to rearrange these sentences into their original, logical order to form a coherent and meaningful paragraph.

Why is it important for competitive exams?

This question type is a pure test of logic and reading comprehension. It assesses a candidate's ability to understand the flow of ideas, identify connections between sentences, and reconstruct a well-structured piece of writing. It does not rely on complex grammar rules but on reasoning skills.

Strategies for Solving Para Jumbles

The key to solving Para Jumbles is not to read the sentences randomly, but to actively look for links and clues that establish a definite order.

Key Strategies & Tricks

  • 1. Find the Opening Sentence: This is the most crucial step. The opening sentence introduces the main topic, a person, or a concept. It is usually a general statement.
    • It will **NOT** start with pronouns that refer to a previous noun (e.g., `he`, `it`, `they`, `this`, `that`).
    • It will **NOT** start with transition words that indicate a continuation (e.g., `but`, `so`, `therefore`, `also`, `however`, `finally`).
  • 2. Identify Mandatory Pairs: After finding a potential opening, look for sentences that have an unbreakable logical link.
    • Pronoun-Antecedent Link: A sentence with a pronoun (e.g., `he`, `its`) must follow the sentence that introduces the noun it refers to. (e.g., A sentence about `Ashoka` must come before a sentence starting with `He...`).
    • Chronological Order: Arrange events based on the sequence of time (e.g., look for dates, or words like `first`, `then`, `next`, `finally`).
    • Cause and Effect Link: The sentence describing a cause will come before the sentence describing its effect (look for words like `because`, `therefore`, `as a result`).
    • Idea-Example Link: A sentence presenting a general idea will come before a sentence that gives a specific example of it (look for words like `For instance`, `For example`).
  • 3. Find the Concluding Sentence: This sentence summarizes the paragraph, provides a final thought, or states a conclusion. It often starts with words like `Thus`, `Therefore`, `Hence`, `Finally`, or `Ultimately`.

    Correct Sequence: CBAD
    Explanation: C provides the main definition and is the best opener. B elaborates on this definition ('It is not merely...'). A further clarifies the definition ('This includes...'). D provides the logical conclusion ('Therefore...').
